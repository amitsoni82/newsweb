How to prevent Mother-to-Child Transmission of Hepatitis B

Written By Amit, July 5, 2024

Health

Mother-to-child transmission of Hepatitis B is a significant public health concern, particularly because the virus can be passed from an infected mother to her baby during childbirth. Hepatitis B is transmitted directly through exposure to infected body fluids, including blood and some other body fluids including semen and vaginal secretions. If left untreated, and if a mother is Hepatitis B positive then the infant has a higher chance of becoming a chronic Hepatitis B carrier which, in time, can result in liver cirrhosis and liver cancer. Thus women need adequate care during pregnancy to mitigate the risk.

Hepatitis B transmission can be prevented only if the disease in pregnant women is diagnosed in the early stages. All pregnant women should be routinely screened for Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) at the first prenatal visit. This test is used to indicate whether the mother is virus-positive and if she is carrying the virus to her fetus. Follow-up tests are then advised for the patients such as HBV DNA quantification to determine the viral load and therefore the likelihood of passing the virus to the baby.

Measures for preventing the spread and transmission of Hepatitis B

Immunoprophylaxis at Birth


The most important procedure for the prevention of mother-to-child transmission includes immunoprophylaxis given to the newborn. This involves administering the Hepatitis B vaccine and Hepatitis B immunoglobulin (HBIG) to the baby within 12 hours after birth. The vaccine tends to provide the person immunity for the long term whereas HBIG in turn gives the protective shield for a short-term basis against the virus.

Follow-up Vaccination Schedule


To be safe the newborn should complete the Hepatitis B vaccination series. This is normally followed by three booster doses at 1 month, 2 months, and 6 months of age. Thus, it is considerable to follow the prescribed schedule of the Hepatitis B vaccine to achieve the prolonged immunity.

Maternal Antiviral Therapy


Sometimes women with high concentrations of Hepatitis B virus (HBV) DNA during the third trimester are encouraged to put on antiviral medications to cut down the viral content even more. The use of antiviral therapy should not be done without consultation of a doctor considering the potential benefits and risks on particular individuals.

Safe Delivery Practices


The mode of delivery (vaginal or cesarean section) does not contribute much to the rates of transmission of Hepatitis B, hence a sterile environment should be maintained, avoiding unnecessary invasive procedures during delivery.

Breastfeeding Considerations


Mothers with Hepatitis B can safely breastfeed their babies once the baby has been given the right immunoprophylaxis at birth. Thus, the advantages of breastfeeding outweigh the modern risk of mother-to-child transmission through breast milk. Ongoing Monitoring and Care


All babies born to Hepatitis B- positive mothers should be monitored constantly to make sure that the virus does not infect them. This entails testing for HBsAg and HBV DNA to assess the efficacy of the vaccine and immunoprophylaxis.

Preventing mother-to-child transmission of Hepatitis B requires a comprehensive approach, starting with early screening and diagnosis during pregnancy. Education and awareness are vital components in preventing mother-to-child transmission of Hepatitis B. Pregnant women should be informed about the importance of screening, vaccination, and adhering to medical advice. Thus, by following the above-mentioned strategies, one can help prevent Hepatitis B infection and its subsequent complications in future generations.
